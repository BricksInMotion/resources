CGI Sci-Fi Weapons (in Carrara)
Article by BuilderBrothers (Edit/Delete/Back to draft)

A tutorial on how to produce fairly impressive sci-fi weapons in Carrara.

Table of Contents
Energy Balls
Lasers

Energy Balls

Before you begin, open Carrara, click “File,” select “New”, and then select “New Empty Doc.”  Next, you click “Insert,” and select “Particle Emitter.”  A settings page should pop up. 

First, change the shape to a sphere, and select the maximum amount of particles you want to be fired.  Next, go to the “Particle Creation” area.  Here, make the number of particles per second about 5, make the size about 0.7, and make the mass 1, the velocity 15, and the dispersion angle 0.  Make all the +/- variables equal to 0.  This makes the speed and direction constant.  Then, go to “Emission Time Range,” and set the amount of seconds in which you want the particles to be created.  It should be from 00.00.00 to something like 00.05.00.  The first number is always 00.00.00 and the second number must always be in between the pair of double zeros.   Then, go down to the left, and select “Die on Impact.” 

Second, go to the top left corner of the settings page and open the “Advanced” tab.  Once in this page, make the air friction equal to -100, the local gravity 0, and the bounce factor and the object’s friction on collisions to 0.  Then, select “Collide with Scene Objects.”  This, along with the “Die on Impact,” makes it so that the particles die (or disappear) when they collide with another object. 

Third, go up past the settings page and select the hand.  This should take you back to the scene, and you should see a cone where your particle emitter is.  Then, select the cone, and go to right of the page where there should be a picture of a gray ball.  Click the “Edit” button next to it.  This should take you to a whole new page, where you see a lot of settings.  Click on the “Color” bar, and make the color whatever color you wish the balls of energy to be.  Then, go down to “Glow”, and again select the color you wish the energy balls to be. 

Fourth, go back to the main page (by clicking the hand again), and go back to the right where the now-colored ball is.  Go above the ball, and click the “Effects” tab.  In this tab, go down to “Aura” and select “Enable.”  Then, click the “Edit” button next to it.  In this settings page, select the color you want the glow around the balls to be.  After doing this, select a radius of about 12 and an intensity of about 25.  Then, click “OK,” and go back to the main page. 

Fifth, go back to the tab and find the “Blur Object” area.  Click “Enable,” and then click “Edit.”  In here, select a radius of about 5, select “Gaussian,” and then “High” under “Quality.”  Hit “OK,” and then go back to the main page. 

Sixth, insert whatever object (like a turret or spaceship) you want to fire the balls, and then line up the emitter to the object by rotating and maneuvering it to that the tip of the emitter points away from the gun. 

Seventh, to make this an animation, move the scrubber to the end of the frames, then go up and move the emitter a little.  This lets the program know that this is an animation and not an image file. 

Eighth, insert the object you want the balls to shoot at.  Make sure the emitter is lined up with the object you want to shoot at, and then go up to the top right of the page and click the “Render” tab (it looks like a film-strip). 

Last, go to the bottom of the page and click “Render.”  Ta-da!  Now you have your very own energy balls!  If you wish to change any of the previously made settings, go right ahead.  Experimenting is a great way to get better at this program!

Lasers

To make a laser in Carrara, you use almost the same settings as when making balls of energy.

The main difference is in the “Particle Creation” area.  Here, make the number of particles per second about 500, make the size about 0.2, and make the mass 1, the velocity 15, and the dispersion angle 0. 

The only other difference besides that is that you must make the “Aura” and “Blur” radii smaller (to about 5), because normally lasers are smaller and thinner than balls of energy.
