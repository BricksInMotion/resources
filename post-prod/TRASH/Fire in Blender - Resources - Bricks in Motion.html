Fire in Blender
Article by Bennett (Edit/Delete/Back to draft)

This tutorial is about making fire in Blender 2.49. I know that I didn't say anything about smoke, but it's the same concept as the fire is.

How to make in Blender 2.49
(This tutorial assumes you have a basic understanding of the buttons and navigation around Blender.)

Introduction

(Just a quick note before we begin! I am using 2.49 in this tutorial because I’m far more comfortable with it then I am with 2.5, and once I get a bit more familiar with 2.5 I will make this tutorial for it also.)

Beginning

Okay, the first and most obvious step is to open up Blender to the default scene. Hit ‘a’ twice and hit ‘x’ to delete everything in the scene. Now we have a blank slate to work with. Then add a circle to your scene. The default 32 vertices is fine, but if you want more or less, it’s all up to you. Select ‘Fill’, and then hit ok and you have your circle in your scene.

Particle System

Second thing’s second, so go to the Particle Buttons in the Object panel. Add a particle system and name it Fire. Change the amount of particles to 6,500 or so, set the start to -50, set the end to 250. Change the life of the particles to 42, and the random to 0.55. You can change these settings to whatever you want them to be, but unless you want the animation to play the flames suddenly bursting up from nowhere, make sure to have the start to be in the negatives enough for the average particle to have already died. Change the particles to emit from Random, because fire is for the most part, well, random. Now, set the random 0.07. So far, it seems like nothing’s really happening, but with this next step you can see the method to my madness. Make the Z acceleration 4.4, and suddenly the particles rise! It’s like magic, except better! Now switch the visualization to object. We’re going to come back to this later, but for now you can ignore it.

    We’re going to be using an object to base our particle system off of. This has multiple advantages: We can animate the size of the object, so the particles will die off as they rise upwards. I got this idea from a tutorial I found once on fire in Blender, and though I though some things they did could have been improved, this was a really awesome idea, and it makes a lot of sense.

Making a Particle

Without further ado, switch your view to layer 2 and add a UVsphere. It doesn’t need to be very high quality; about 6 segments and 6 rings will be just fine. Make it set smooth, and do whatever else you want to do with it, then go over to the Materials panel. Add a new material, and make the color of the object the same color you want your fire to be. I’m going to make green fire in this tutorial, because quite frankly I’m really tired of rendering out lots of orange fires. Make the specularity color a lighter version of that color, just for a bit better look, though again, it really won’t matter at all because when we composite the flame you won’t be able to tell the difference. Make the alpha low, about 0.3, and enable Z transparency. Another good idea I got from the tutorial is to enable VCol lighting. Make the specularity and harness very high, because fire is bright. An important thing to do is to keep in mind that we are basing all of these physical attributes off of real fire. If you’re ever stuck, think...”What would real fire look like?”

    Now hit ‘n’ to bring up the transform properties, and make the X, Y, and Z scales of the object 0.02. Insert a scale keyframe, and move up about 1/3 of how long the average particle lasts. We set ours to 32, so go up to frame 14 or 15 and change the scale value to 0.1 and add another keyframe. Then go to frame 42, the last frame, and make the particle very small, about 0.001, or possibly something even smaller. Keyframe it again, and you have your animated particle. You can adjust the curve of the IPOs if you want to, but I think that with what we’re doing it’s just more work than it’s worth.

Putting it Together

From here, go back to layer 1 and select the circle spewing out particles again. Under the particle buttons, make the object name whatever you named your UVsphere. Now play back the animation and see what you have. If it looks like the particles die out too quickly, then just adjust the life and keyframes for the particles.

    Now add a lamp, and make the brightness about 1.1, and change the distance amount to 5,000. Then position the lamp wherever you want it to be. I put mine a bit up and to the right, so that the particles would be lit at an angle.

    Add a camera. Position it wherever you want, it’s really just a matter of personal preference. I want to have a straight on shot, so I moved it back on the Y axis and a bit up on the Z axis. Go to the Render Panel now, and change sky to key, RGB to RGBA, and make the image type .png so that it can handle transparency. Then specify you camera size. Hit render, and...”This isn’t fire. Hey, you tricked me BG! You said this was a tutorial about creating fire, not a bunch of spheres!”

Compositing

That may be correct, be we’re not finished yet. If you have After Effects or another program that you want to do your compositing in, awesome! You get to do the last bit in there and exit Blender after you render your video. But if you still want to use Blender for the compositing, then go to the node editor, go to Composite Nodes, and hit Use Nodes. Then you can fiddle around with warping the image and re-layering it on with the Mix node (switched to add) that will make up about half you nodes. Once you’ve fiddled around with it and have something you like, render out a test frame, then if you still like it, render the animation.

    The reason we made it over a transparent background is that if you used your background movie sequence/picture, when you applied the nodes you would distort the raw movie clip you imported. The solution, if you want to do it in Blender, is at the end of your nodes, put an image/movie node in, and put the fire in front of it. Turn on the Do Composite button, and you’re set in silver and ready to render it out!

Conclusion

You might notice in this tutorial I’m not exact about a lot of things, and the main purpose of this is to get you to think about how you want to do this yourself, and to create something unique to you and your project. Toy around with the particle settings, number of particle systems, material, and nodes and see what you come up with!
